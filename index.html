Experiment 1: Study the I-V Characteristics of a PN Junction Diode

This experiment focuses on understanding and plotting the current-voltage (I-V) characteristics of a PN junction diode under both forward and reverse bias conditions. This is fundamental to understanding diode behavior in electronic circuits.

Aim

To study and plot the forward and reverse bias I-V characteristics of a PN junction diode.

Apparatus Required

  • PN Junction Diode (e.g., 1N4007)
  • DC Regulated Power Supply (0-30V, variable)
  • Milliammeter (0-50 mA)
  • Microammeter (0-50 μA)
  • Voltmeter (0-1V for forward bias, 0-30V for reverse bias)
  • Breadboard
  • Connecting Wires

Theory (Click to expand)

A PN junction diode is a two-terminal semiconductor device that allows current to flow predominantly in one direction. It is formed by joining P-type and N-type semiconductor materials.

Forward Bias: When the positive terminal of the external DC voltage source is connected to the P-side and the negative terminal to the N-side, the diode is said to be forward biased. This connection reduces the width of the depletion region and lowers the potential barrier. Once the applied voltage exceeds the knee voltage (approximately 0.7V for Silicon and 0.3V for Germanium), the diode starts conducting heavily.

Reverse Bias: When the negative terminal of the external DC voltage source is connected to the P-side and the positive terminal to the N-side, the diode is said to be reverse biased. This connection increases the width of the depletion region and strengthens the potential barrier. A very small leakage current (reverse saturation current) flows due to minority carriers. If the reverse voltage is increased beyond a certain limit, called the Zener breakdown voltage or avalanche breakdown voltage, the diode breaks down and conducts heavily in the reverse direction, which can damage the diode if current is not limited.

The diode current (I) as a function of voltage (V) is given by the Shockley diode equation:
I = I_0 ( e(qV / ηkT) - 1 )

  • I0 is the reverse saturation current
  • q is the charge of an electron (1.6 x 10-19 C)
  • V is the voltage across the diode
  • η is the ideality factor (typically 1 for Germanium, 1 to 2 for Silicon)
  • k is Boltzmann's constant (1.38 x 10-23 J/K)
  • T is the absolute temperature in Kelvin

Procedure (Click to expand)

A. Forward Bias Characteristics

  1. Connect the circuit as shown in the forward bias circuit diagram.
  2. Set the DC power supply voltage to zero.
  3. Slowly increase the forward voltage (VF) in small steps (e.g., 0.05V or 0.1V).
  4. For each step, record the corresponding forward current (IF) and voltage (VF).
  5. Continue increasing voltage until current reaches a significant value (e.g., 20-30 mA).

B. Reverse Bias Characteristics

  1. Connect the circuit as shown in the reverse bias circuit diagram.
  2. Set the DC power supply voltage to zero.
  3. Slowly increase the reverse voltage (VR) in steps (e.g., 0.5V or 1V).
  4. For each step, record the corresponding reverse current (IR) and voltage (VR).
  5. Continue increasing voltage until breakdown or max safe voltage.

Experiment 2: Design an Amplifier using BJT

This experiment involves designing a common emitter (CE) amplifier using a Bipolar Junction Transistor (BJT). The focus is on understanding biasing, calculating gain, and observing the amplifier's characteristics, including its frequency response.

Aim

To design and implement a common emitter (CE) amplifier using a Bipolar Junction Transistor (BJT) and study its voltage gain and frequency response.

Theory (Click to expand)

A BJT in common emitter configuration provides voltage, current, and power gain. Proper biasing (e.g., voltage divider bias) sets the Q-point in the active region. The voltage gain (Av) is approximately -RC/re', where re' is the AC emitter resistance. The negative sign indicates a 180° phase shift.

Experiment 4: Design of Active Low Pass Filter using Op-Amp

This experiment focuses on designing a first-order active low-pass filter. Such filters pass low-frequency signals while attenuating high-frequency signals. The "active" part, using an Op-Amp, provides gain and buffering.

Theory (Click to expand)

An active low-pass filter uses an Op-Amp to provide gain and isolate the filter from load effects. For a first-order filter, the cutoff frequency (fc) is 1/(2πRC). The passband gain for a non-inverting configuration is 1 + Rf/Ri.
